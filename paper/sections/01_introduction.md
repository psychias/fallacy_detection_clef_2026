# 1. Introduction

Online discussions on platforms like Reddit are a productive testbed for fallacy detection: the texts are short, the topics range across politics, science, and lifestyle, and the rhetorical moves include the full inventory of informal fallacies that classical argumentation theory catalogued long before social media [Sahai et al. 2021; Walton, Reed, Macagno 2008]. The Touché 2026 lab at CLEF formalises this as a shared task and runs it on the Reddit-derived corpus introduced by Sahai et al. [Sahai et al. 2021; Heinrich et al. 2026].

The lab is organised as three subtasks. ST1 is binary: given a comment together with its parent and title context, decide whether the comment commits an informal fallacy. ST2 is an eight-way classification over the fallacious subset, with labels authority, black_white, hasty_generalization, natural, population, slippery_slope, tradition, and worse_problems. ST3 is a four-way classification over the non-fallacious subset, distinguishing four Walton-style argument schemes along a goal axis (practical vs. epistemic) crossed with a basis axis (internal vs. external) [Macagno 2022]; we abbreviate the four labels as pi, pe, ei, ee throughout this notebook. Each subtask is run in two tracks: a base track that uses only the original Reddit fields, and an enhanced track that additionally exposes organiser-rewritten paraphrases (text_enhanced, argument_enhanced). The enhanced fields were produced with knowledge of the gold labels, which we address explicitly in Section 3 and Section 6.

We submit one run per subtask and track, totalling six TIRA runs [Fröbe et al. 2023]. All six are RoBERTa-large encoders fine-tuned with task-specific heads, trained with a small amount of LLM-generated synthetic contrastive data, and in the enhanced track for ST2 and ST3 additionally extended with pseudo-labelled unlabelled examples. We chose this configuration after running a wider grid of backbones and recipes, and after a 5-fold cross-validation comparison on ST3 against an LLM-based alternative.

This notebook makes three contributions. First, a per-subtask ablation that isolates the marginal effect of synthetic augmentation and pseudo-labelling on F1-macro. Second, a backbone comparison that reports what worked, what failed, and what looked competitive but did not survive cross-validation, covering RoBERTa-base/large [Liu et al. 2019], DeBERTa-v3-base/large [He et al. 2023], ModernBERT-large [Warner et al. 2024], and four Qwen variants from 3B parameters to 32B [Qwen Team 2024; Qwen Team 2025] fine-tuned with LoRA / QLoRA [Hu et al. 2022; Dettmers et al. 2023] and chain-of-thought supervision [Wei et al. 2022]. Third, an honest negative-results record: DeBERTa-v3-large NaN explosions, an LLM detour that did not beat the encoder under cross-validation, and a practical-external (pe) rare-class ceiling that survived targeted augmentation and per-class threshold calibration. We believe the third contribution is the most useful one for teams considering the same design space next year.
