# 6. Discussion

## 6.1 Why the encoder beat the LLM at this scale

Across the cells we were able to validate under cross-validation, encoder fine-tuning outperformed every LLM track we evaluated. The most informative comparison is on ST3 enhanced: RoBERTa-large 5-fold CV reached 0.875 (exp_0153), Qwen2.5-7B CoT-LoRA reached 0.851 ± 0.114 (exp_0180) on the same fold split, and Qwen2.5-32B single-run reached 0.841 (exp_0207) without a completed CV. The Qwen-7B std overlaps the encoder mean, so the apparent 0.024 gap is not statistically meaningful from a single 5-fold split; the strongest honest summary is that the LLM track did not show a benefit at this dataset scale, not that LLMs are categorically worse. We are careful to scope the claim to this dataset (~473 ST3 examples), this label structure, and our compute budget; we would not extrapolate to larger labelled corpora or to richer reasoning targets.

## 6.2 The enhanced-track leakage question

The 0.97 ST2-enhanced and 0.91 ST1-enhanced numbers (exp_0072, exp_0035) are the single biggest credibility risk in this paper, and we treat them in four steps.

*(a) The concern.* The enhanced-track inputs text_enhanced and argument_enhanced were rewritten by the organisers with knowledge of the gold labels. Any classifier trained on those fields has direct access to features that an honest pipeline would have to extract from raw text, and the leakage source is structural rather than accidental.

*(b) A sanity check, and what it does not establish.* We ran a regex-mask sanity check (exp_0062) that replaced known fallacy-name strings and obvious cue words in the enhanced fields and re-trained the ST2-enhanced classifier on the masked inputs. F1 was retained at 0.937 after masking, against the unmasked enhanced-track baseline of exp_0036 (0.937). The mask deliberately covers the eight ST2 fallacy-class names and their morphological variants only, not the full semantic content of the rewritten fields; a null delta is therefore the expected outcome *if* the classifier was not relying on those literal strings as a shortcut, and is the specific hypothesis the check rules out — not a general claim that the rewritten fields contain no label-carrying signal at all. We label this a sanity check rather than a diagnostic for exactly this reason: it rules out a hypothesis the rewriter's design made a priori unlikely, and therefore provides limited positive evidence about the harder semantic-leakage channel.

*(c) The interpretation.* A retained F1 of 0.937 under aggressive string masking means that trivial string-matching of fallacy labels in text_enhanced is not the dominant signal the classifier relies on. That is useful evidence against the most superficial leakage story, but it is a narrow result, and the defence of the enhanced-track headline numbers therefore rests primarily on the commitment in part (d) rather than on this sanity check. We cannot rule out softer leakage from the rewriter's semantic choices — a paraphrase produced with label knowledge can carry the label in word choice, sentence structure, hedging, or topical framing without ever using a fallacy-name string.

*(d) The commitment (primary defence).* **Therefore**, we present base-track and enhanced-track numbers separately throughout this notebook and do not aggregate them into a single headline. Where the paper takes a position — e.g., on the ablation pattern, on the encoder vs LLM comparison, on the pe ceiling — we anchor that position on the base-track behaviour or on the 5-fold CV numbers, not on the enhanced-track point estimates. We note that the ST2-enhanced per-class F1 values (Appendix B) cluster between 0.948 and 1.000 with one cell at perfect 1.000 — a tight distribution that is itself consistent with the leakage concern raised in (a).

The leak-audit gate of 4.5 (exp_0081) sits inside the same framing: it is one additional layer of defence specifically against the pseudo-label step amplifying any leakage that survived the rewrite, and it returned no detectable leak under the test we ran. We name the limit of that gate explicitly too — it tests against the same kind of signal the masking diagnostic tests against, not against the softer semantic channel.

## 6.3 What pe taught us about rare-class limits

The practical-external (pe) class on ST3 was the binding constraint on macro-F1 throughout the project. The NLI-style basis probe (exp_0082) found pe-F1 of 0.25 well before we trained the final classifier, and a zero-shot Qwen2.5-3B baseline (exp_0091) reached pe-F1 of 0.0 on the same partition. We responded with three interventions: a pe-targeted synthetic batch of 100 (pe+practical-anchor) pairs (exp_0103), a per-class threshold sweep that pushed the pe threshold all the way down to 0.05 (exp_0145), and an LLM basis-axis meta-learner (exp_0146) that aggregated encoder logits with an external reasoning signal. The threshold sweep gave +1.0 pp aggregate F1, the synthetic batch contributed to the final ST3-enhanced number, and the meta-learner gave no improvement. The practical-external cell sits at F1 = 0.573 on dev (precision 0.563, recall 0.583, n=24, exp_0073), against 0.766–0.810 for the other three ST3 classes; the pe ceiling is a concrete 0.19–0.24 F1 gap that the targeted synth augmentation (exp_0103), the per-class threshold sweep (exp_0145), and the basis-axis meta-learner (exp_0146) did not close. We read the residual gap as a finding rather than a failure: at the dataset scale we are working with (~473 ST3 examples, with pe as a small minority class), the rare-class limit appears to be more about the small evidence base than about pipeline shortcomings, and pushing past it likely requires either more annotated pe examples or a sharper formal characterisation of the practical-external scheme than the one our classifier currently encodes.
